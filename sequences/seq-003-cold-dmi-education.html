Part 1 — The Direct Open — Day 1
Single version
From Roland Mercer To {{first_name}} Subject Comparing how banks handle inherited deposits, yours included

{{first_name}},

I'm Roland. I work with Prismm, infrastructure that captures beneficiary designations at account origination, so when an account holder dies, the institution already knows who inherits the account instead of losing that relationship by default.

We built a scoring tool, the Deposit Mortality Index, using only public data, FDIC branch deposits, Census demographics, Federal Reserve holdings data, SSA life tables, to estimate how much of a bank's deposit base is scheduled to leave through mortality over the next ten years. Almost none of the institutions we've talked to can tell us what share of their account holders have a named, reachable beneficiary on file. It's not a hard number to pull. It's just that no one has been assigned to own it.

That gap shows up as scheduled runoff, not a one-time loss. Every account holder who dies without a plan in place is a household that eventually banks somewhere else, and internally it just reads as a closed account.

Reply "yes" and I'll walk you through what this looks like for {{organization_name}}.

Roland

Part 2 — Growth Coverage Ratio — Day 6
Single version
From Roland Mercer To {{first_name}} Subject Your growth rate isn't what it looks like

{{first_name}},

Following up on the beneficiary question from my last note.

Banks typically look at growth one way: this year's deposit total against last year's. The DMI growth ledger measures it against something else, trailing three-year deposit growth against annualized mortality-driven runoff, the rate deposits leave because account holders died, regardless of anything the team does. We call the relationship between those two the Growth Coverage Ratio.

In our twelve-bank pilot cohort, mortality-driven runoff added up to 6.6 to 8.6 percent of total deposits over ten years. If {{organization_name}}'s trailing growth isn't clearing the annualized version of that number, the deposit base isn't actually expanding, it's replacing what mortality was always going to take, and every dollar spent on acquisition is partly just backfilling that loss.

That reframes customer acquisition cost. Replacing a depositor who died with no plan in place is a different, more expensive problem than keeping the relationship with their heirs, and most banks are paying that cost without measuring it.

Reply and I'll build {{organization_name}}'s Growth Coverage Ratio from public data, no submission required.

Roland

Why this version
First metric anchor: Growth Coverage Ratio, defined against the actual published mechanic (trailing three-year growth vs. annualized mortality runoff) rather than an invented year-over-year formula. Ties directly to CAC, the metric this audience already tracks. CTA is a direct offer, no false urgency.

Metric anchorGrowth Coverage Ratio — trailing three-year deposit growth vs. annualized mortality-driven runoff. CAC reframe lives here.

Restart Triggers

  • They reply to any part of the sequence
