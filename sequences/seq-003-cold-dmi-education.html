Part 1 — The Direct Open — Day 1
Single version
From Dean Whitfield To {{first_name}} Subject Comparing how banks handle inherited deposits, yours included

{{first_name}},

I'm Dean, and I work with Prismm. We help banks and credit unions keep deposits in the family when an account holder dies, by making it simple for beneficiaries to stay instead of moving their money elsewhere.

I'm reaching out because we've been talking to banks across the country while building a report on this exact problem, and almost none of them can tell us what share of their account holders have a named, reachable beneficiary on file. Not because it's a hard number to pull, but because no one has ever been assigned to own it.

Our early findings put the cost of that gap at hundreds of millions in lost deposits over the next decade for a mid-size bank, and into the billions for larger institutions. Every account holder who dies without a plan in place is a household that eventually ends up banking somewhere else, and it never shows up as a loss internally. It reads as a closed account.

Is your bank currently tracking this?

Dean

Why this version

Part 2 — Growth Coverage Ratio — Day 6
Single version
From Dean Whitfield To {{first_name}} Subject Your growth rate isn't what it looks like

{{first_name}},

Following up on the beneficiary question from my last note.

Most banks measure growth against last year. We measure it against something else: how much of your deposit base is scheduled to leave through mortality, meaning depositors passing away, regardless of anything your team does. We call the ratio between the two your Growth Coverage Ratio.

Across the twelve-bank cohort we've studied, mortality-driven runoff alone ran 6.6 to 8.6 percent of total deposits over ten years. If your reported growth doesn't clear that bar, you're not actually growing. You're refilling a bucket with a hole in it, and every dollar spent on acquisition is partly just replacing deposits you were always going to lose.

That reframes customer acquisition cost. Acquiring a new depositor to replace one who died with no plan in place is strictly more expensive than keeping the relationship with their heirs, and most banks are paying that cost without knowing it.

Want us to calculate your Growth Coverage Ratio using public data? No submission required on your end.

Dean

Why this version
First metric anchor: Growth Coverage Ratio. Ties directly to CAC, the metric this audience already tracks, rather than introducing a new one. CTA is a direct offer to run the analysis for their institution, matching how a banker would actually want to respond.

Metric anchorGrowth Coverage Ratio — reported growth vs. mortality-driven runoff. CAC reframe lives here.

Using public FDIC deposit data, Census demographics, and actuarial life tables, we can estimate a bank's mortality-driven runoff over ten years with reasonable precision, no internal data required. For a representative mid-size institution, that number lands in the hundreds of millions. For larger banks, it moves into the billions.

That's not attrition from rate shopping or a competitor's promotion. It's depositors dying with no beneficiary plan in place, and their heirs banking somewhere else by default. It happens on a schedule, which is what makes it different from every other kind of deposit loss you're used to modeling.

Part 4 — Succession Capture Deficit — Day 18
Single version
From Dean Whitfield To {{first_name}} Subject The 15 percent of the score most banks are failing

{{first_name}},

We score a bank's exposure across four components:

  • Actuarial exposure, 50 percent of the score. Your deposit-weighted depositor age.
  • Composition fragility, 20 percent. How concentrated your deposits are in a narrow set of account types or households.
  • Market concentration, 15 percent. How reliant you are on a single branch footprint or region.
  • Succession capture deficit, 15 percent. Whether your institution has any operational capability to keep a relationship once the account holder dies.

That last one is the only component that isn't about your depositor demographics. It's about process.

Private banking solved this decades ago with trust officers whose job is exactly that moment. Retail banking never built the equivalent, so on this component specifically, nearly every institution we've scored comes back weak, regardless of size or market. It's also the one part of the score fully within a bank's control to fix.

Want to see where you'd land on it?

Dean

Why this version
Third metric anchor: succession capture deficit, with all four components broken into a scannable list rather than buried in a sentence, since this audience is skimming. Ends on the one component fully within the institution's control, which is the strongest hook of the four.

Metric anchorSuccession capture deficit, 15% of the composite score. The one component that's about process, not demographics.
